Chapter One

Introduction

This chapter frames the Nawa Sons Dairy Farm Milk Production Information System by describing the farm context, the challenges of manual processes, and the objectives, scope, and limitations for the proposed solution.

1.0 Overview

Nawa Sons Dairy Farm seeks to modernize operations through a centralized digital platform that unifies milk collection, storage, sales, and customer engagement. Manual notebooks and handwritten receipts have sustained the farm for years, yet they fall short in today’s data-driven agricultural landscape. The documentation adopts a sequential structure— literature review, methodology, analysis, design, and implementation—to capture the full lifecycle from problem discovery to deployment.

The introductory chapter provides the background, problem statement, objectives, significance, scope, and limitations. These components speak directly to managers, employees, and stakeholders who rely on accurate records, efficient workflows, and modern customer service to remain competitive.

1.1 Background Information

Nawa Sons is a family-owned farm that prides itself on delivering fresh milk. Quality, timeliness, and efficiency are non-negotiable, yet the team depends on manual production and sales processes. Milk quantities are logged in notebooks, sales get handwritten receipts, payments are cash-based, and reconciliations occur manually—creating gaps between recorded sales and actual cash, and obscuring accountability.

Inventory checks rely on estimates, causing shortages or wastage, while supplier coordination depends on phone calls and rough projections. Customer engagement lacks a structured system for loyalty or purchase histories, and promotions are difficult to evaluate. As industry peers adopt computerized solutions for production monitoring, inventory control, and sales management, Nawa Sons must digitize to stay competitive. The proposed system digitizes production data, automates inventory, streamlines sales, and embeds customer relationship management to deliver accurate, real-time insight.

1.2 Statement of the Problem

Manual record-keeping introduces reconciliation errors between production, stock, and revenue. Inventory checks lack consistency and miss restocking cues, disrupting service delivery. Financial accuracy suffers from manual calculations, and management cannot generate timely reports or analyze customer behaviour, limiting loyalty initiatives and marketing strategies. These inefficiencies reduce profitability and compromise customer satisfaction. The proposed information system automates production tracking, inventory monitoring, sales processing, and embeds analytics for data-driven decisions.

1.3 Objectives

General Objective: Design and implement a computerized information system that improves efficiency, accuracy, and managerial insight across Nawa Sons Dairy Farm.

Specific Objectives

  1. Digitize milk production records with real-time monitoring of collection and storage.
  2. Automate sales transactions with integrated payment options and electronic receipts.
  3. Deliver detailed reporting for production, inventory, sales, and financial analysis.
  4. Provide customer management tools for tracking preferences and buying trends.
  5. Improve supplier communication through integrated order management.
  6. Reduce operational delays by streamlining workflows and minimizing manual interventions.

1.4 Significance and Relevance

Automating previously manual processes reduces errors, saves time, and enhances the customer experience. Accurate reporting and real-time data empower managers to forecast demand, plan production, optimize inventory, and strengthen financial accountability. The project mirrors broader agricultural technology trends, offering tangible benefits to owners, employees, and customers alike.

1.5 Scope

  • Milk Production Tracking: Daily yield recording, storage monitoring, and quality control.
  • Inventory Management: Automated stock monitoring, reorder alerts, and supplier records.
  • Sales Processing: Transaction logging, payment integration, and digital receipts.
  • Reporting: Production, inventory, sales, and financial dashboards for analysis.
  • Customer Management: Preference tracking, purchase histories, and loyalty metrics.
  • Supplier Communication: Embedded order management to streamline procurement.

Future enhancements may introduce online ordering, delivery fulfillment, and mobile applications for remote engagement.

1.6 Limitations

Key challenges include upfront hardware/software costs, the need for reliable internet for cloud-enabled features, and staff training commitments. Change management is critical to overcome resistance from teams accustomed to manual workflows. External factors—seasonal milk supply fluctuations, for example—remain outside the system’s control, but the long-term gains in efficiency and competitiveness outweigh these constraints.

Chapter Two

Literature Review

The literature survey establishes theoretical grounding, evaluates existing agricultural information systems, inspects applicable software methodologies, and highlights the gaps the Nawa Sons solution intends to close.

2.1 Introduction

The review synthesizes theories, platforms, and tools relevant to dairy production management, drawing on sources such as O’Brien and Marakas (2019) to underscore the value of aligning system design with contemporary technological trends.

2.2 Theoretical Framework

  • Technology Acceptance Model (TAM): Guides usability and perceived usefulness so staff can adopt the platform with minimal friction.
  • Systems Theory: Treats the farm as an interconnected ecosystem, emphasizing seamless data flow between production, inventory, sales, and reporting.
  • Information Systems Success Model: Prioritizes system, information, and service quality to drive user satisfaction and organizational impact.

2.3 Review of Existing Systems

FarmLogs: Excels at crop tracking but lacks dairy-specific modules such as milk yield and herd health monitoring.
Afimilk: Provides advanced analytics but demands significant infrastructure investment, pricing out smaller farms.
iCow: Offers mobile guidance for livestock care but omits integrated inventory and sales management.

The Nawa Sons platform differentiates itself through an affordable, end-to-end feature set tailored for small and medium dairy operations.

2.4 Review of Methodologies

The Waterfall model provides linear predictability but struggles with evolving requirements. The Spiral model prioritizes risk mitigation but can be resource intensive. Agile stands out for its iterative cadence, stakeholder feedback loops, and ability to incorporate emerging priorities—making it the preferred choice for the dairy farm project.

2.5 Technological Trends and Tools

  • Languages & Frameworks: Python, Django, and JavaScript deliver flexible backend/frontend capabilities.
  • Databases: PostgreSQL and MySQL remain reliable for structured agricultural datasets.
  • Security: SSL/TLS encryption and hashing algorithms (e.g., SHA-256) protect sensitive records.
  • Emerging Trends: Cloud hosting and mobile responsiveness boost accessibility and scalability.

2.6 Gaps in the Literature

  • Limited customization for small farms with unique workflows.
  • Dependence on continuous internet connectivity, challenging in rural contexts.
  • Weak reporting and analytics in existing platforms.
  • High licensing and infrastructure costs.

The proposed system addresses these by providing customizable modules, offline-friendly workflows, robust reporting, and cost-conscious deployment.

2.7 Critical Analysis and Synthesis

Literature emphasizes agile, user-centered systems (Pressman & Maxim, 2020; Sommerville, 2020). Existing farm platforms improve record keeping but fall short in automation, affordability, and usability. The synthesis informs a secure, adaptable design rooted in the theories presented earlier.

2.8 Conclusion and References

By applying TAM, Systems Theory, and the IS Success Model—implemented through Agile practices and modern web tooling—the Nawa Sons system aims to deliver an efficient, user-friendly platform that bridges the gaps identified in existing literature.

Chapter Three

System Analysis

Chapter Three focuses on the Agile methodology applied, feasibility findings, data collection instruments, and functional/non-functional requirements shaping the build.

3.1 Introduction

The analysis validates that the envisioned platform is practical, aligns with stakeholder needs, and establishes a strong foundation before moving into design and implementation.

3.2 Systems Development Methodology

Agile SDLC underpins the build: iterative sprints (2–4 weeks), continuous feedback from farm managers, flexibility for requirement shifts, and close collaboration between developers and stakeholders. Phases include requirements analysis, design (DFDs/ERDs/UI mockups), implementation, testing (unit, integration, UAT), deployment with training, and ongoing maintenance.

3.3 Feasibility Study

Item Estimated Cost (KES)
Software Development 250,000
Hardware Upgrades 120,000
Staff Training 60,000
Maintenance 40,000

The farm’s existing laptops and connectivity satisfy technical feasibility. Economically, projected first-year efficiency gains of KES 140,000 yield:

ROI = (140,000 / 470,000) × 100 ≈ 30%

Operational feasibility is ensured via intuitive interfaces and staff training; schedule feasibility targets a six-month delivery (two months analysis/design, three months development/testing, one month deployment). Legal compliance aligns with Kenya’s Data Protection Act (2019), and the system reduces paper dependency, supporting environmental feasibility.

3.4 Requirements Gathering

Interviews with managers, surveys across 30 employees, on-site observation, and ledger reviews uncovered recurring errors in manual records, reconciliation delays, lack of customer tracking, and supplier communication gaps. These insights directly informed functional requirements for automation, alerts, analytics, and CRM capabilities.

3.5 Data and System Analysis

Functional Requirements

  • Daily milk production tracking with yield histories.
  • Inventory management plus automated reorder alerts.
  • Sales processing with integrated payments and receipt generation.
  • Customer preference tracking and loyalty analytics.
  • Comprehensive reporting across production, inventory, sales, and finance.

Non-Functional Requirements

  • Usability: simple, intuitive interfaces.
  • Reliability: accurate data storage and retrieval.
  • Security: role-based access and encryption of sensitive data.
  • Scalability: capacity for future modules like online ordering.
  • Performance: responsive data entry and reporting.

System Specifications

  • Hardware: standard laptops/desktops with internet access.
  • Software Stack: Python, Django, PostgreSQL, JavaScript, HTML/CSS.
  • Database: centralized relational store with backup strategy.
  • Primary Users: managers, sales staff, inventory clerks, and administrators.
